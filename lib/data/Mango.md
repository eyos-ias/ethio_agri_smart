# WHAT'S MANGO? 
Mango is a tropical fruit known for its sweet and juicy flavor. It belongs to the Anacardiaceae family and is native to South Asia, although it is now cultivated in many parts of the world. Mangoes come in various shapes, sizes, and colors, ranging from green to yellow, orange, or red when ripe. The fruit has a smooth outer skin and a fibrous inner flesh surrounding a large, flat seed.

# Name
1. Scientific name:Mangifera indica.


2. common name:mango.

# Characteristics
Plant Type: Mango is a tropical fruit tree.

Mature Size: Mango trees can grow up to 30 meters (98 feet) in height

Sun Exposure: Mango trees thrive in full sun

Soil Type: Mango trees prefer well-draining soil that is rich in organic matter.

Soil pH: slightly acidic to neutral soil pH range of 5.5-7.5.

Bloom Time: Mango trees typically bloom in late winter to early spring.

Color: The flowers of mango trees are usually small and white or pale yellow in color.

Hardiness Zones: 10-12

Native Area: Mango trees are native to South Asia, specifically India, Myanmar, and Bangladesh.

Toxicity: The fruit of the mango tree is not toxic and is safe for consumption.

# How to grow Mango?
To grow a mango tree, here are some general steps to follow:

1. Climate and Location: Mango trees require a warm tropical or subtropical climate. Ensure that your location has a minimum temperature of 30°F (-1°C) and is frost-free.

2. Soil Preparation: Prepare the soil by ensuring it is well-draining and rich in organic matter. Amend the soil with compost or well-rotted manure to improve its fertility and drainage.

3. Planting: Plant a grafted mango tree rather than growing from seed, as it ensures a true-to-type variety. Dig a hole that is wide and deep enough to accommodate the root ball of the tree. 

4. Watering: Water the newly planted tree thoroughly and regularly, especially during the establishment period. 

5. Fertilization: Mango trees benefit from regular fertilization. Apply a balanced fertilizer specifically formulated for fruit trees according to the manufacturer's instructions.

6. Pruning: Prune the mango tree to maintain its size and shape, promote airflow, and remove dead or diseased branches

7. Pest and Disease Control: Monitor the tree for common pests like aphids, scales, and fruit flies. Use appropriate organic or chemical controls if necessary.

8. Harvesting: Mangoes typically take several months to ripen after flowering. Harvest the fruit when it reaches its mature size, color, and aroma. 


# Light
Mango trees require full sun exposure to thrive and produce abundant fruit. They need a minimum of 6-8 hours of direct sunlight per day. Lack of sufficient sunlight can result in poor growth, reduced fruit production, and increased susceptibility to diseases. Therefore, it is important to plant mango trees in a location where they can receive ample sunlight throughout the day.
# Soil

Mango trees prefer well-draining soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5. They can grow in sandy, loamy, or clay soils, but good drainage is important to prevent root rot. Adding organic matter like compost or manure can improve soil fertility.

# Water
Mango trees require regular watering, especially during the growing season. Here's a shorter summary of their water requirements:

Mango trees need consistent watering, especially during the growing season. Keep the soil evenly moist, but avoid overwatering to prevent root rot. Water deeply and infrequently, allowing the top few inches of soil to dry out between waterings. Adjust watering frequency based on weather conditions and the tree's water needs.

# Temperature and Humidity
Mango trees thrive in warm and humid conditions. Here's a shorter summary of their temperature and humidity requirements:

Mango trees prefer temperatures between 70°F and 85°F (21°C to 29°C). They can tolerate brief periods of temperatures as low as 40°F (4°C) but are sensitive to frost. High humidity levels, ideally between 50% and 70%, are beneficial for mango trees. However, they can still grow in lower humidity environments with proper care and irrigation.

# Ferilizers
Mango trees benefit from regular fertilization to support healthy growth and fruit production. Here's a shorter summary of their fertilizer requirements:

Use a balanced fertilizer with a ratio of nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K), such as a 10-10-10 or 14-14-14 formula. Apply the fertilizer three times a year: in early spring, early summer, and early fall. Use a slow-release fertilizer or organic compost to provide a steady supply of nutrients over time. Follow the manufacturer's instructions for application rates based on the tree's size and age. Additionally, consider supplementing with micronutrients like iron, zinc, and magnesium if deficiencies are observed. Regularly monitor the tree's health and adjust the fertilizer program as needed.

Here are some possible fungal diseases that millet may encounter:

1. Head Smut (Sphacelotheca spp.): Head smut is a common fungal disease that affects millet. It causes the formation of black, powdery masses of spores on the inflorescences (panicles) of the plant. These spores can spread to healthy plants, leading to reduced grain quality and yield.

2. Ergot (Claviceps spp.): Ergot is a fungal disease caused by species of the Claviceps genus. It produces black, elongated structures called sclerotia on the millet panicles. These sclerotia can replace the seeds, reducing yield and quality. Ergot-infected grains may also contain toxic alkaloids, posing a risk to humans and livestock if consumed.

3. Downy Mildew (Sclerospora spp.): Downy mildew is a fungal disease that affects various crops, including millet. It manifests as yellow to orange lesions on the leaves, leading to wilting and drying out. Infected panicles may also show discoloration and reduced seed set.

4. Rust (Puccinia spp.): Rust is a fungal disease that causes orange to reddish-brown pustules on the leaves, stems, and panicles of millet. Severe infections can result in premature leaf senescence, stunted growth, and reduced grain production.
## Bacteria
Here are some possible bacterial diseases that millet may encounter:

1. Bacterial Leaf Streak (Xanthomonas spp.): Bacterial leaf streak is a common bacterial disease that affects various grass crops, including millet. It is caused by Xanthomonas bacteria. It primarily affects the panicles of millet, leading to blighted florets and discolored seed heads. 2. Crop Rotation: Practice crop rotation to break disease cycles and reduce the buildup of pathogens in the soil. Avoid planting millet or other susceptible crops in the same field year after year. Rotate with non-host crops to disrupt disease progression.

3. Field Sanitation: Maintain good field hygiene by removing and destroying crop debris after harvest. Disease-causing pathogens can survive on plant residues, so clearing them away helps reduce the risk of infection in subsequent plantings.

4. Proper Irrigation and Drainage: Ensure proper irrigation practices tailored to the specific needs of millet. Over-irrigation or waterlogging can create favorable conditions for diseases to thrive. Adequate soil drainage is essential to prevent waterlogged conditions.

5. Weed Control: Weeds can act as reservoirs for diseases and provide a breeding ground for disease-carrying insects. Implement effective weed control measures, such as manual removal, mulching, or herbicide application as needed.

6. Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Implement an IPM approach to manage insect pests that can transmit diseases. Monitor insect populations regularly, employ cultural practices to discourage pests, and consider targeted insecticide applications when necessary.

7. Nutrient Management: Maintain proper nutrient levels in the soil through balanced fertilization practices. Well-nourished millet plants are better equipped to withstand diseases and are less susceptible to infections.

8. Vigilant Monitoring: Regularly inspect your millet fields for any signs of diseases, such as leaf discoloration, wilting, or abnormal growth.

# Pests
Here are some common pests that can infest mango trees:

1. Mango Fruit Fly (Bactrocera dorsalis): The mango fruit fly is a major pest of mangoes. The female fruit fly lays eggs in the fruit, and the larvae feed on the pulp, causing fruit damage and premature drop.

2. Mango Seed Weevil (Sternochetus mangiferae): The mango seed weevil attacks the seeds of mango fruits. The adult weevils lay eggs inside the fruit, and the larvae feed on the seed, leading to fruit damage and reduced seed viability.

3. Mango Shoot Borer (Chlumetia transversa): The mango shoot borer is a serious pest that attacks the shoots and branches of mango trees. The larvae tunnel into the shoots, causing wilting, dieback, and reduced growth.

4. Mango Hopper (Idioscopus spp.): Mango hoppers are sap-sucking insects that feed on the leaves and shoots of mango trees. Heavy infestations can lead to leaf curling, yellowing, stunted growth, and reduced fruit production.

5. Mango Mealybug (Drosicha mangiferae): Mango mealybugs are small, soft-bodied insects that produce a waxy covering. They suck sap from leaves, shoots, and fruits, causing leaf yellowing, premature leaf drop, and honeydew secretion.

6. Mango Aphid (Aphis gossypii): Mango aphids are small, soft-bodied insects that feed on the sap of mango leaves and shoots. They can cause leaf curling, stunted growth, and the secretion of honeydew, which can lead to the growth of sooty mold.

7. Red-Banded Thrips (Selenothrips rubrocinctus): Red-banded thrips are tiny insects that feed on mango leaves, causing distorted growth, silvering of leaves, and reduced fruit quality.

8. Scale Insects: Various scale insects, such as mango shield scale (Aspidiotus destructor) and mango soft scale (Coccus mangiferae), can infest mango trees. They attach themselves to the leaves, branches, and fruits, sucking sap and weakening the tree.


# Pesticides
 Here are some common pesticides used in mango production:

1. Insecticides: Insecticides are used to control a wide range of insect pests that affect mango trees. Some commonly used insecticides for mango include:

   - Organophosphates (e.g., chlorpyrifos, malathion): These insecticides are effective against pests like mango hoppers, mango shoot borers, and aphids.
   
   - Pyrethroids (e.g., deltamethrin, cypermethrin): Pyrethroids are broad-spectrum insecticides used to control pests such as mango hoppers, fruit flies, and thrips.
   
   - Neonicotinoids (e.g., imidacloprid): Neonicotinoids are systemic insecticides that are absorbed by the tree and provide control against pests like mango leafhoppers and aphids.

2. Fungicides: Fungicides are used to manage fungal diseases that commonly affect mango trees. Some commonly used fungicides for mango include:

   - Copper-based fungicides: Copper-based fungicides, such as copper oxychloride or copper hydroxide, are used to control diseases like anthracnose, powdery mildew, and bacterial spot.
   
   - Triazoles (e.g., tebuconazole, propiconazole): Triazoles are systemic fungicides effective against diseases such as anthracnose and powdery mildew.
   
   - Strobilurins (e.g., azoxystrobin, pyraclostrobin): Strobilurins are broad-spectrum fungicides used for controlling diseases like anthracnose, powdery mildew, and downy mildew.

3. Herbicides: Herbicides are used to control weeds that compete with mango trees for resources. Some commonly used herbicides for mango include:

   - Glyphosate: Glyphosate is a non-selective herbicide used to control a wide range of weeds. It is often used for weed management around the base of mango trees.
   
   - Pre-emergent herbicides: Pre-emergent herbicides, such as oxyfluorfen or pendimethalin, are used to prevent weed germination and growth before they emerge in the orchard.

# Toxicity
There are a few considerations regarding the potential toxicity of certain parts of the mango tree:

1. Skin and Sap: The sap and skin of mangoes contain a substance called urushiol, which is also found in poison ivy and poison oak. Some individuals may be sensitive or allergic to urushiol, and contact with the skin or sap of mangoes can cause dermatitis or an allergic reaction in these individuals.

2. Mango Leaves: Mango leaves contain compounds called tannins, which in large quantities can be toxic to humans. Ingesting significant amounts of mango leaves may result in stomach discomfort or other gastrointestinal symptoms. It is advisable to avoid consuming large quantities of mango leaves.

3. Mango Peel: While the flesh of a ripe mango is safe to eat, the peel is generally not consumed due to its tough texture. In some cases, the peel may cause irritation or discomfort if ingested in large amounts.
# Harvesting
Here is a simplified overview of the mango harvesting process:

1. Fruit Maturity: Mangoes should be harvested when they are fully mature but still firm. The exact maturity stage varies depending on the mango variety, but generally, ripe mangoes exhibit a change in skin color and emit a pleasant aroma.

2. Visual Inspection: Inspect the mangoes visually to identify those that are ready for harvest. Ripe mangoes often have vibrant skin color, such as shades of yellow, orange, or red, depending on the variety.

3. Twist and Pull: Gently twist the mature mangoes and give them a slight upward tug. Ripe mangoes should detach easily from the tree, indicating that they are ready for harvest.

4. Harvesting Tools: In commercial mango orchards, long poles with harvesting baskets or bags attached to the end are commonly used for picking mangoes that are out of reach. These tools allow harvesters to reach higher branches and collect the fruits without causing damage.

5. Handling with Care: Handle the harvested mangoes with care to avoid bruising or damaging the fruit. Mangoes have delicate skin, and rough handling can cause blemishes or affect their quality.

6. Sorting and Packing: After harvest, sort the mangoes based on their size, shape, and quality. Remove any damaged or diseased fruits. Clean and pack the mangoes in appropriate containers, such as crates or boxes, ensuring proper ventilation to prevent moisture buildup.

7. Storage and Transportation: Depending on the destination and market requirements, the harvested mangoes may be stored in a cool, well-ventilated area for a short period or transported to processing facilities or markets for sale.

# Pruning 
Here is a simplified overview of the pruning process for mango trees:

1. Timing: Pruning is typically done during the dormant season, which is after the fruit has been harvested and before new growth begins. The exact timing may vary depending on the specific climate and growing conditions of the region.

2. Tools: Use sharp and clean pruning tools, such as pruning shears or loppers, to make clean cuts and minimize the risk of disease transmission.

3. Removal of Dead or Diseased Wood: Start by removing any dead, damaged, or diseased branches. Cut these branches back to their point of origin or to a healthy lateral branch. Dispose of the pruned material properly to prevent the spread of diseases.

4. Thinning: Thin out overcrowded areas by selectively removing branches to improve airflow and light penetration throughout the tree canopy. This helps reduce the risk of fungal diseases and promotes better fruit development.

5. Shaping and Size Control: Prune to maintain the desired shape and size of the mango tree. Remove excessive vertical growth or branches that are crossing or rubbing against each other. Maintain an open center or vase-shaped structure to allow sunlight to reach the inner parts of the tree.

6. Water Sprout Removal: Water sprouts are vigorous vertical shoots that emerge from the trunk or main branches. Remove these sprouts as they divert energy from fruit production and can weaken the tree structure.

7. Pruning After Fruit Production: In some cases, selective pruning can be done after fruit production to stimulate new growth and encourage branching. This can help improve fruiting in subsequent seasons.


# What time is best for pruning of mango?
 In general, the following guidelines can help determine the ideal time for pruning mango trees:

1. Post-Harvest: Wait until the mango fruit has been harvested before initiating pruning. This allows the tree to allocate its energy towards fruit production and ripening.

2. Dormant Season: Prune mango trees during the dormant period when the tree is not actively growing. In tropical and subtropical regions, this is often in the cooler months of winter or early spring. The exact timing may vary based on the local climate and specific mango variety.

3. Avoid Frost: Make sure to avoid pruning during periods of frost or when cold temperatures are expected. Pruning during freezing conditions can damage the tree and inhibit its recovery.

By pruning during the dormant season, the mango tree has an opportunity to recover and heal from the pruning cuts before entering the active growth phase. It also allows for better visibility of the tree's structure and facilitates the removal of dead, damaged, or diseased branches.


# Crop Distance
The spacing requirements may also differ based on the intended tree size at maturity and the specific orchard management system being employed. However, as a general guideline, here are some spacing recommendations for mango trees:

1. High-Density Planting: For high-density orchards, where mango trees are kept smaller through regular pruning and training, spacing between trees can range from 10 to 20 feet (3 to 6 meters) apart in both directions. This close spacing allows for efficient land utilization and higher yield per unit area. Pruning and training techniques help manage the tree size and maintain light penetration.

2. Standard Planting: In traditional or standard mango orchards, where trees are allowed to grow to their natural size, spacing requirements are typically wider. Depending on the variety, mango trees are often planted 25 to 40 feet (8 to 12 meters) apart in both directions. This wider spacing provides ample room for the trees to develop a full canopy, access sunlight, and ensure air circulation.

3. Dwarf or Semi-Dwarf Varieties: Certain dwarf or semi-dwarf mango varieties are available that naturally have smaller tree sizes. These can be planted closer together, typically around 10 to 15 feet (3 to 4.5 meters) apart, to maximize production in limited spaces.
